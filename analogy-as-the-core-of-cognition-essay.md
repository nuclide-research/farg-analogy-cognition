# Analogy as the Core of Cognition

When I was eight years old, I fell in love with exponents. I cannot defend this. I can only report it. I made tables of integers raised to powers and I filled whole notebooks with them, and the little raised number perched up to the right of the big one struck me as one of the most thrilling things in the universe. It told the big number to do something. It made it grow.

One day I was wandering through our house and I passed a paper my father was reading. He was a physicist, and the page was a thicket of symbols I could not begin to follow. But one detail jumped out at me. Tucked down to the lower right of certain letters were tiny numbers. X-sub-one. X-sub-two. And I thought: there it is again. If a little number up high tells you to raise the big one to a power, then surely a little number down low tells you to do some other secret thing. What operation does the subscript perform? I was electrified. I went and asked my father.

He looked up and said, kindly enough, that it does not do anything. It is just a way of naming variables. And I was flattened. Completely, completely flattened. The magic drained out of the page. I had wanted a hidden machine, and I had been handed a filing system.

Now jump forward forty years. I am a grown man, a father, sitting on the playroom floor watching my one-year-old daughter Monica, who has discovered the Dustbuster. She is pressing its button and the thing roars to life, and she is having the time of her life. Then her eyes find a second button. Of course she presses it. Of course she expects another roar, some new and greater power. She presses it, and nothing happens. She presses it again. Nothing. So I get up and go over to her, and I press it myself, and a little hatch swings open, and out comes the dust compartment. This, I show her, is just where the garbage goes, sweetheart. And Monica is, of course, destroyed.

And in that instant, sitting on the floor, the whole episode with my father came rushing back to me. The subscripts, the exponents, the flattening, all of it, whole and entire. I had not thought about that scene in something like forty years. It had been lying somewhere in my head all that time, with no name and no label, waiting. And what hauled it up out of the dark? A baby and a vacuum cleaner. Look at the shape of it. Little Doug makes an analogy, superscript so therefore subscript, reaches for a hidden operation, and gets variable-naming instead. Monica makes an analogy, one button so therefore the other, reaches for hidden power, and gets the garbage compartment instead. Two reachings, two flattenings, fused across four decades by what I can only call a shared essence.

I want you to sit with what just happened in my head, because I think it is the whole story of cognition in miniature.

For a long time the field I work in has had a habit I find quietly maddening. It chops thinking into pieces, and it gives each piece a tidy name, and somewhere toward the back of the list, usually bundled in with problem solving, sits a small specialty labeled "analogical reasoning." I have taken to saying that this makes analogy into the Delaware of cognition. A real state, certainly, there on the map, but tiny, and off to one side, and not where anybody imagines the action is.

I want to do something more daring than promote it to Indiana, or California, or Texas. I want to throw out the official picture entirely. First, let us be rid of the word "reasoning." Analogy has almost nothing to do with reasoning. That pairing is a misnomer wrapped around a misconception. And as for the geography: analogy is not a state at all. Analogy is the interstate freeway system of cognition. It is what links everything to everything. It is not a small zone off to one side. It is the whole network of roads. (I came up with that line only yesterday, and I am rather pleased with it, so you will have to forgive me for leaning on it.)

Here is how the pieces fit. Categorization is the name of the game. The work of a mind, moment to moment, is deciding what things are, putting a finger on the essence of each situation it meets. And analogy is the mechanism that makes categorization happen. To borrow the corny little form they print on aptitude tests, analogy is to thinking as a motor is to a car. Now, I confess I distrust those A-is-to-B-as-C-is-to-D puzzles, shoe is to foot as glove is to hand, because they cram analogy back into a tiny box. But take the motor seriously. It is not a feature of the car you admire on weekends. It is what makes the car a car that goes.

So let me try, not to define analogy, because I hate definitions and am bad at them, but to point at it. Analogy making is the perception of a shared essence between two things. Two footnotes, to keep me honest. I do not mean some glowing philosophical essence that things possess in themselves. I mean the essence you perceive at a particular moment, in the particular frame of mind you happen to be in. And when I say "two things," it is tempting to picture two objects out in the world. But the analogy does not live out there. It lives in your head. It is a connection drawn between two mental representations, two concepts. We then project that connection outward and announce that the boat and the hurricane are alike. Reasonable enough. But the event itself happened between two structures inside a single skull.

Let me show you what that constant inner traffic looks like, because it is far stranger and far more relentless than people suppose. Most analogies serve no purpose whatever. They are not tools we reach for. They simply happen, all day long, and then they vanish.

A few months ago I was reading a book my mother had lent me, by Jonathan Raban, about a voyage across the Atlantic. In the first chapter he sails from Liverpool toward Canada aboard a great ship called the Atlantic Conveyor. He is awed by the sheer size of the thing and assumes nothing could rock it, but the captain corrects him: a ship is a vulnerable creature. And sure enough a hurricane comes churning up the Atlantic, dead in their path. The captain, with marvelous nonchalance, calls it a "whirly girl." So the ship slides a little south, lets the storm pass to the north, and curves back on course. A small episode, a few pages, not even the heart of the chapter. I read it on a plane flying home from California, and we landed in Denver to change planes.

I got off and started down one of those endless concourses. A woman was crossing in front of me, towing a suitcase, and I swerved a little to the left to avoid her. And in that swerve, bang: I am the boat, she is the hurricane, she is the whirly girl, and this corridor is the Atlantic Ocean. Two seconds standing in for two days. Now here is the part I most want you to notice. That little event happened so fast, and meant so little, that I would never have caught it if I were not such an inveterate spy on my own thinking. It would have flashed and vanished and left no trace on the world. The only reason it has left a mark is that it is now lodged in your minds too. Your skull is filled, every waking second, with thousands of these flickering, purposeless analogies. They are the medium you think in.

Now consider how analogy slowly stretches a single concept until it covers ground its first owner never imagined. Take the word "shadow." There is the obvious one: a lovely oak at my parents' ranch, casting its dark pool of shade under a summer noon. But here is a photograph of the same tree in winter, on an overcast day, with no sun at all, and there is still a dark patch on the ground beneath it. That patch is the absence of snow. The snow fell, but the tree caught it, so the ground beneath stayed bare. A snow shadow. Look at what just happened to the word. A shadow no longer needs light. It needs only something blocked.

Then there is the rain shadow. East of the Cascades in Oregon the mountains stop the clouds, and for a couple hundred miles there is desert, because the rain never makes it over. And my favorite. I was talking with an Italian friend whose brother teaches in the far north of Norway, and I said the cold up there must be brutal. Not at all, he said. It is rather mild, because the Gulf Stream runs warm and runs north. But the cold part, he said, is southern Norway. How can that be, I asked, so much farther south? Because England sits in the way and blocks the warm current. So England casts a shadow of the Gulf Stream onto the Norwegian coast. No light, no snow, now no rain, only the same shape of an essence: something casts, something is blocked, something is missing behind it. The word has traveled a very long way from a tree at noon, and every step of the way was an analogy.

While I had snow on the brain, I let myself make a small foolish leap. We know snow is made of flakes. There is an analogy between light and snow. So perhaps light is made of flakes too. Call it the light-flake hypothesis. A suitably light and flaky hypothesis, I grant you. Hold onto it. It comes back at the end with better credentials.

There is a deeper claim hiding under all this, and I want to put it plainly: there is no fundamental difference between a single memory trace and a category. Between one stored episode and a full-blown concept. They are the same kind of thing. Watch how readily a single unique entity, given a little time, begins to leak until it is a whole class. There may be a young Einstein in this room. Poor Milton Babbitt, whatever you think of him, is no Mozart. What if the next Chopin were born in Lompoc? Some novel is hailed as the next Catcher in the Rye. A war becomes the Soviet Union's Vietnam. Beirut was once the Paris of the Middle East, and Jerusalem, I have heard, is a mecca for tourists, mecca now with a small m. A traitor is a quisling, small q, after one particular Norwegian minister who threw in with the Nazis and whose name slid down into a common noun. And Galileo, training his telescope on Jupiter, saw little points of light beside it and called them moons, lowercase, equating them with our one solitary Moon. A magnificent leap. He took the word for the only such object anyone had ever conceived of and made it plural, and a unique thing in the universe became a category. That is pluralization, and it is analogy doing its quiet work.

Our minds have one more gift that runs the whole machine: an unlimited talent for chunking. We take a handful of small concepts, set them in some relationship, draw a membrane around the bundle, and then the contents go all but invisible. We are left holding a clean new box that we wield without ever looking inside. Think of an airline "hub." Buried inside that one tidy word is a child's whole education. You needed ball, which rolls. You needed roll, which gives wheel, and wheel, which gives spoke and hub and the very idea of a center. You needed vehicle, built from bike and bus and car, and from car the plane, and from plane the airliner, and from airliner the airline. You needed names, then brands, then companies. You needed a trip broken into legs and formalized into routes, hung on a network of nodes. You needed buying and money and downsizing. And I have not even mentioned city or airport. Dozens of concepts, learned across years, all folded into that one membrane. Yet when someone says Denver is a United hub, you think about none of it. You reach for the chunk whole, and that is exactly the point. We think with soap opera and sleazeball and laissez-faire economics and ethnic cleansing and Bose-Einstein condensation and the Wikipedia and pork-belly futures and phishing spelled with a ph. Try explaining the Wikipedia to someone from two thousand years ago and count the levels you would have to build first. A dog has no inkling of these. A four-year-old has no inkling of these. And we handle them with no effort whatsoever, and that ease is the whole miracle.

And please notice, while we are here, that I have been favoring nouns and visible things, and that is not where the action is. Most of our categories are neither visual nor objects nor nouns at all. Consider the word "please." It names no object. It names a circumstance, a class of situations in which that word is the right one. A three-year-old who wants a cookie is in one. I, standing here, am not. Consider "well," whatever part of speech that even is. There are well-dot-dot-dot situations, and a native speaker knows them in his bones, so that if a foreigner drops "well" where no well-situation calls for it, the wrongness rings out instantly. The same goes for "probably," and for the fine, real line between a probably-situation and a maybe-situation. A colleague I respect enormously once told me categorization at this level is just a matter of feature detectors, and I thought, we are not on the same planet. Tell me the feature detectors that fire when I recognize a mess. Tell me the visual features of "well." There are none. There are situations, and we judge membership by analogy and nothing else. The richest of these are the phrases we carry, what Joe Becker called the phrasal lexicon, a mental store far larger than any list of single words. Speak of the devil. Back to square one. What is sauce for the goose is sauce for the gander. Sometimes a whole proverb boils down to one word: sour grapes, which I see with a hyphen and hear as a single word, what Roger Schank rightly called a situation label, a one-word stamp for a whole human predicament. We match the situation in front of us to the proverb by analogy, and the match feels like nothing at all.

But it is not nothing underneath. Every word you choose is the visible tip of a buried fight. When my friends Kelly and Dick visited, they kept referring to my "office," and I kept bristling, because to me it was obviously my "study." My office is across campus. We finally talked it out. In their house the top floor with the books and the computers, the place where they work, is the home office, so mine was an office too, plainly. But I grew up watching my father, who had a study on the upper floor of our house, with his Friden calculator and his books, and an office down on campus. Each of us had a childhood-home analogy running silently underneath, and each of us found his own word a no-brainer. Word choice is steered by analogy every single time, even when the steering is invisible.

Sometimes the steering becomes audible, and then it is glorious. The competing words collide and fuse, and a blend pops out. Somebody phones asking whether my son Danny is home, and I say "I'll go sec," see and check welded into one. Danny himself, marvelously, overloaded one day, protested, "I can't keep all these in my bread," brain and head fused at once. To his sister heading to the store: "Wait up, I'll gum with you," come and go in a single syllable. Tired and wanting the sack, I once worried in writing that my editor would "hit the stack," a glorious three-way pileup of hit the sack and blow his stack and hit the ceiling. And a dean of mine, trying to recruit a star, declared he would "pull no stops unturned." Every one of these is a recording, made audible, of a battle that is usually fought in perfect silence. When the battle is a landslide, you hear nothing, and the word arrives clean. When the race is close, the evidence pours out: a stretched consonant, a bent vowel, a blend.

Which brings me, at last, to the highest reach of the same machinery, the stroke of genius, and to a discovery that astonished me only recently, when I was preparing a talk on Einstein's analogies for the centenary of his miraculous year. I had never understood where the photon came from. Einstein himself called the light quantum the single most revolutionary idea of his life. And it came from an analogy between two curves.

The first was the black-body spectrum. Trap radiation in a hot cavity and the energy distributes itself in a hump, a little at long wavelengths, a little at short ones, a great mound in the middle, a bell-shaped curve. The second came from Maxwell and Boltzmann, and it described an ideal gas, which everyone pictured as a swarm of molecules caroming around in a box. Work out how their speeds distribute and you get another hump, few molecules slow, few fast, a great many in the middle, another bell-shaped curve. Both curves had been lying in plain sight for years, in different rooms of physics, and nobody had set them side by side. Einstein set them side by side and said, simply, this one looks like that one. And then he reasoned the only way that mattered. The gas curve has that shape because the gas is made of particles, of molecules bouncing around. The radiation curve has the same shape. So perhaps light, too, is made of particles. He did the elegant work, computed the entropies, and out came the light quantum. The most revolutionary idea of his life, born from noticing that one bell-shaped curve resembled another.

Which is, you will forgive me, exactly the move I made between my snow shadow and my summer shadow, the move that handed me the light-flake hypothesis as a joke. A bell-shaped curve and a bell-shaped curve, a snow shadow and a summer shadow, two things set side by side until a shared essence flashes between them. Einstein made the move a hundred years before I made mine, in dead earnest, and was right. He simply put his finger on a far deeper essence than I did.

So here is where forty years of circling this question have left me. A stroke of genius is an analogy. A reminding that surfaces after forty years is an analogy. The recognition of a hurricane in a passing traveler is an analogy. A word stretched to a new shadow, a proper name leaking into a category, a chunk wielded whole, a "please" placed correctly, a proverb fitted to a situation, a slip of the tongue, the silent choice between study and office: every one of them is an analogy. So I will ask the question that has organized my life. If strokes of genius and personal insights and dinner-table remarks and random remindings and instantaneous categorizations and the slips and blends are all made of the same single thing, of one mind perceiving a shared essence between two of its own representations, then might not all of cognition be made of analogies? That is the core. Analogy is not the Delaware of the mind. It is the freeway carrying every thought you have ever had, including this one.
